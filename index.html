01 — Philosophy

Design Thinking

Core values & approach

The thing I keep coming back to is cognitive load. When I'm evaluating a design idea — even a good one — my first question is always: does this add mental work, or remove it? A feature that looks clever in isolation can quietly destroy an experience when it's placed in a real, messy context.

I work through a divergence → convergence loop on every project. During divergence I try to suspend judgement completely — bad ideas are useful because they define the edges of the problem. Convergence is where the real work happens: I hold each remaining idea against physical constraints, available hardware, and the non-negotiable needs of the person actually using it.

What this term taught me is that the visual channel is already oversubscribed. In GP2 I saw firsthand how adding one more overlay to Blender's interface made things worse, not better. I've started actively asking which sense is currently underused — and whether audio, haptics, or peripheral cues could do the job less intrusively. Design, at its best, feels like a quiet partner.

A Family of Buttons

Four group members, one shared theatre stage. Each button tells a personal story through a slideshow — and at the end, they all celebrate together. I proposed the theatre concept and built the "Kosi" button, with a volleyball icon reflecting my interests in sports, gaming, and food.

Divergence — Early Sketches

In Subgroup B01-4, before we'd even agreed on a concept, I was sketching buttons that grow when clicked, buttons that roam the canvas, and buttons that vanish for a few seconds and come back. None of those made it through — but the exercise got me thinking about personality-as-behaviour rather than personality-as-colour.

Final Implementation

The theatre concept was mine — I pitched it in our first joint session and it stuck. Dark stage, red velvet curtains, four buttons below. My "Kosi" button uses a volleyball icon and its slideshow covers the things that actually represent me: sports, gaming, and food. The group celebration at the end — applause sign lighting up for every member — was a deliberate choice to show what we shared, not just what made us different.

My Contribution & Reflection

I proposed the theatre metaphor in our first joint session — the stage, the curtains, buttons as performers. My "Kosi" button used a volleyball icon and a slideshow covering the things that actually represent me — sports, gaming, and food. WebRTC · Multi-Modal Spatial Audio · ML Mar 2026
Marco Polo — Expertise Sharing

A real-time Blender guidance tool that keeps novices fully inside their own workspace — no second screen, no visual overlays, just spatial audio pings and edge-glow cues to point them in the right direction.

↗
Divergence — What We Ruled Out & Why

We generated a wide list: hot/cold cursors, gaze-tracking heatmaps, ghost trails, haptic trackpad feedback. My main contribution during divergence was consistently pushing back on visual overlays. Blender's interface is already dense — every extra layer we added made things worse, not clearer. I kept asking: which sense is currently unused here? That lens is what led us to spatial audio for 3D targets.

Final Application & My Role

The expert drops notes tagged as World Space or UI Element; the novice says "Marco" to trigger a spatial audio ping that gets higher-pitched and centre-panned as they move closer. I helped scope the project to Blender (instead of TinkerCAD) based on what we could realistically build, and I defined the tagging system. I also shaped the code architecture — breaking implementation into phases so the team could work in parallel.

The honest version: Whisper had a two-second delay that weakened the core interaction, the edge-glow for UI Element mode never shipped, and the Teachable Machine frustration detector flickered unreliably. Strong concept, rough execution — that gap is a real lesson about embodied systems needing much more time for hardware reliability.

My Contribution & Reflection

My contributions fell across three areas. During divergence and brainstorming, I was one of the more active voices — I consistently pushed back on ideas that added visual clutter to Blender's already dense interface, which helped steer the group toward spatial audio as the primary guidance channel. I also helped scope the project to Blender over TinkerCAD based on what we could realistically build.

On the technical side, I was involved in debugging the codebase as features came together. And I wrote and compiled the group's submitted write-up — pulling together each member's design decisions, the system spec, the core interaction loop, the known limitations, and the overall reflection into one coherent document.

What I'd do differently: treat Whisper as a first-week risk spike, not a feature to build around. The two-second delay wasn't a tuning problem — it was a mismatch between real-time guidance and an async transcription pipeline. Catching that earlier would have freed us up to use push-to-talk instead.

"Different sensory channels offer different precision — and that precision has to match the spatial task. Audio works for 3D. It doesn't work for finding a 10-pixel button. That distinction changed how I think about multi-modal design."

The Prompt Cup

A 3D-printed, sensor-equipped cup with an OLED screen and colour-coded LEDs that eases small talk at social events. I explored four concepts, rejected all of them, and those rejections are what shaped the criteria I brought to the group.

↗
My 4 Concepts — All Rejected

I started from two angles: how do you get people to socialise more, and how do you make the eating experience itself better? The Conversation Plate died because the display surface gets covered in food. The Energy Checker Sphere felt more like surveillance than a social tool. The Pacing Plate & Cup risked publicly embarrassing someone for eating too fast — which is the opposite of making dinner fun. The Temperature Bowl was complex without solving the core socialising problem.

None of those moved forward as the group's choice, but working through their failure gave me a clear evaluation framework: the object has to be naturally held and mobile, the sensing surface can't be the eating surface, and the system must give people a reason to approach each other, not just react to the environment.

My Contribution & Reflection

When the group converged on David's Prompt Cup I contributed the evaluation framework that validated it: mobile object, clean sensing surface, reason to seek out other people. Beyond that, I pushed for the mic-based LED timing — the idea that the blink reminder should delay if the microphone detects active conversation, and trigger sooner if the room goes quiet. The goal was a system that responds to whether the interaction is working, rather than blindly running on a clock.

I was also involved in the deliberation over what a "prompt" should be — a broad topic like Sports, or a direct question like What's the best sci-fi film you've seen this year? We landed on direct questions because they give people an immediate jumping-off point instead of leaving them to fill in all the substance themselves.

The technical constraint I'd have pushed back on earlier: the cup shape took so long to design and print in Fusion 360 that we only ever tested one form factor. The Weisen style worked, but we never found out if a narrower goblet — which Elina's sketch suggested — would have kept the LEDs more visible during use.

"My four rejected concepts weren't wasted work — they gave me a precise set of criteria to evaluate David's Prompt Cup against. It passed all of them immediately, which is exactly what a good group convergence should feel like."

03 — Growth

Overall Reflection

Shift · 01
Visual Isn't Always the Answer

Starting this course I defaulted to visual solutions for everything. By GP2 I was actively arguing against adding visual overlays. I now treat audio, haptics, peripheral cues, and embodied metaphors as legitimate first-class tools — and I reach for them before I reach for another UI element.

Shift · 02
Rejection Is Part of the Process

In GP3, none of my four concepts survived to the group meeting as the chosen design. That used to feel like failure. Now I see it as the divergence phase working correctly — you're supposed to produce ideas that don't make it, because they sharpen the ones that do.

Shift · 03
Document the Gaps, Not Just the Wins

GP2's Whisper delay, the flickering frustration detector, the edge-glow that never shipped — writing those down clearly was harder than writing about what worked, and more valuable. A design philosophy only comes through when you're honest about the distance between intention and execution.

Looking · Ahead
What I Want to Carry Forward

I want to keep working on embodied interaction — physical objects as interfaces, not screens. The Prompt Cup showed me how much richer an interaction becomes when it lives in your hand. My next goal is to get better at prototyping physical form early, before committing to electronics, so I can actually test whether the object feels right before the circuit board is soldered in.
